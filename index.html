Introduction

Technology jobs are on the rise and have become some of the most coveted jobs in our society, particularly software development roles. Although diversity in technology companies is frequently discussed today, most tech companies are still struggling to hire and keep women and other minority groups in their companies. This explorable explanation hopes to give you a glimpse into the harsh reality women in tech face.

So, what now?

We built a game that will feature some of the main reasons women leave tech. We'll show you how to play it.

Pay Gap

“The gender wage gap is smallest among candidates in their 20s, and grows as they increase in age.”

“Women just starting off in their careers both expect and receive salaries that more closely align with the salaries offered to men with similar years of experience.”

Let's dig deeper...


Men are offered higher salaries than women for the same job at the same company 63% of the time

Women are offered 4% less than men on average for the same role at the same company

More than half of women know they’ve been paid less than men in similar roles throughout their careers

Additionally, as women get older, they’re also more likely to have children, which is also linked to lower salaries.


The situation is worse for women of color or those who identify as LGBTQ.


Source: Hired 2018 Wage Inequality Report


Lack of awareness, access, and exposure to opportunities

Roughly ⅓ of men (32%) say discrimination against women is not a problem (PEW)

Let's dig deeper...


“A 2013 analysis found that half of female STEM professionals leave the field within 12 years, compared with less than 20 percent of women in other professional fields...A 2016 report from the National Center for Women and Information Technology (NCWIT) blames the retention issue on ‘workplace conditions, a lack of access to key creative roles, and a sense of feeling stalled in one’s career.’”


Source: The Verge


Poor culture around work-life balance

A survey of over 1,000 women in tech found that 83% of mothers felt pressured to return early from maternity leave. 38% of respondents cited a fear of losing credibility or value. 34% said they were directly pressured by colleagues or managers. 32% of the women feared losing their jobs.

Source: Indeed

Passed up for promotion

“52% of women leave technology as an industry because they’re made to feel unwelcome and undervalued, and see their careers stagnating”

Source: Alphr

Let's dig deeper...


30% of underrepresented women of color were passed over for promotion - a amount considerably higher than White or Asian women and all other groups of men.

Source: 2017 Tech Leavers Study


“ There was a multi-year pattern of young affluent white males who lacked skills and experience being unfairly sponsored, fast-tracked through promotions, and handed top choice of high profile projects. I was forced to compete on an uneven playing field where opportunities for growth and advancement were unlikely. I left the company for a more diverse and inclusive organization ”
- Chinese, Female, Engineer

Source: Alphr

1 in 3 women in the Elephant in the Valley (EITV) survey reported feeling afraid of their personal safety because of work related circumstances
